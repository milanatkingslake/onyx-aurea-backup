Why use work tickets?

Although you can use the Work Tickets feature for many purposes, two typical uses are to:

  • Track the discovery and resolution of product errors, using the work ticket as a quality assurance tool.

Although a product error or enhancement request can result from a sales, service, or support incident, the issue might not be resolved at the same time that the incident is resolved and closed. You can create a work ticket to track work on the issue after the incident is closed.

  • Track a project that affects multiple customers.

When a project must be completed for many customers, you can manage the entire project from a main source, the work ticket, while the task for each customer is tracked and managed by an individual incident.

Example: Product quality assurance

Suppose a customer reports a problem with “View 2.1,” a software product your organization sells. The support technician creates a support incident for the customer and assigns it to the support engineer to investigate the nature of the problem. On investigation, the support engineer discovers the problem has resulted from a product flaw, so he creates and supplies a patch to the customer. Having resolved the customer’s immediate problem, the support engineer closes the support incident. However, because he has identified a flaw in the product, he creates a work ticket, links it to the product View 2.1 by that product’s association to the incident product “Assistant Tools,” and assigns it to a development engineer.

Once the support engineer creates the work ticket, the engineering group can prioritize the problem and track its resolution. The engineer can update the work ticket, saving work notes about the issue, options for fixing it, and final resolution. He can record the priority, status, and severity of the issue in the work ticket, while the audit-log automatically saves the history of changes to these attributes over the life cycle of the work ticket.

In addition, at any point in the cycle, end users can associate the work ticket with the customer who identified the error, with the support incident that was created for the customer, or with both.

Using this combination of incident and work ticket, the customer’s immediate problem can be addressed on a separate time line from resolution of the product error. In this case, your company might supply the customer with a software patch several months prior to releasing the next version of the product that incorporates the same fix.

Example: Multiple customer project

Suppose, as a marketing associate, you must contact all of your customers that currently own a product to market to them a newer version of that product. You can accomplish this by creating a work ticket to organize and track the project as a whole, and then create associated sales or service incidents for each customer that you must contact. When all of the associated incidents have been closed, you can close the work ticket, completing the project.

One benefit to organizing the project in this manner is that you can delegate the work of contacting the customers to other marketing associates by assigning the individual sales or service incidents to them, while you maintain ownership of the project, as represented by the work ticket.
